Modular 4 DoF 3D Printed Robotic Arm

A Journey into Innovation and Modularity

S C R O L L

In the ever-evolving world of robotics, innovation often springs from the minds of passionate individuals. This is the story of a solo journey into a captivating project—a Modular 4-DOF (Degrees of Freedom) 3D Printed Robotic Arm. As the sole member of the team, this endeavor became a testament to innovation, determination, and the power of modern manufacturing techniques.

A Solo Quest for Versatility and Affordability

A Singular Objective: My primary goal was straightforward—to create a modular robotic arm that would provide a versatile, cost-effective, and adaptable solution for various industries and applications. The vision was to ensure reliability, efficiency, and sustainability in every aspect. The crowning achievement would be the arm's modularity, allowing for the attachment of any tool for enhanced versatility.

From Concept to Creation

This ambitious project required a one-person orchestra, combining mechanical ingenuity with cutting-edge technology:

Design for Manufacturability (DFM): Every component had to be meticulously designed to be 3D printed with ease. I paid particular attention to minimizing the need for supports, especially in critical areas like precision holes, to ensure efficient manufacturing.

Design for Assembly (DFA): Simplifying the assembly process was crucial. With only screws, nuts, and force fits, the entire arm could be assembled, eliminating the need for specialized assembly equipment.

Section 1: Planetary Gear Drive - Crafting Precision and Power

I delved into the world of involute gear design principles, applying this knowledge to parametrically model external and internal gears. This innovation amplified the base plate torque and precision by 4X.

I chose herringbone ("V-shaped") gears for the planetary gearbox due to their silent, smooth, and efficient operation.

The central location of the sun gear in the planetary drive allowed for an improved wire management system, streamlining wire routing.

Section 2: Cycloidal Gear Drive - A Compact Powerhouse

I developed a compact, fully 3D printable cycloidal drive to increase the torque of the first vertical joint by a factor of 13.

Countless iterations were made, adjusting parameters such as eccentricity and roller radius to develop a reliable, smooth, and efficient drive.

I strategically selected the cycloidal drive over other types for its higher accuracy, load capacity, transmission efficiency, and compactness.

Section 3: Bevel Gear Differential Joint - Efficiency through Design

I applied my knowledge of spur gears to design a parametric bevel gear set, approximating the tooth profile of the bevel gears using Tredgold’s approximation.

Section 4: Belt Drives - Transmitting Torque Across Distances

I converted linear dimensions from a GT2 timing belt into radial dimensions and applied them to model a parametric GT2 timing belt gear.

Parametric models created two sets of GT2 timing belt gears for torque transmission between distant parallel shafts.

Section 5: 3D Printable Cross Roller Bearings - Precision without the Price Tag

I designed a parametric cross roller bearing model in SolidWorks, saving over $1000 by eliminating the need to purchase large-sized industrial-grade cross roller bearings.

The choice of a cross roller bearing was deliberate, as it excels in handling both axial and radial loads.

Section 6: Structural Components - Efficiency in Printing

To reduce print time and filament cost, I removed the need for supports on small horizontal holes by adding a 40-degree tip, ensuring they remained above the minimum printable angle threshold of 30 degrees.

I conducted trials to determine the average shrinkage rate of various 3D printing filaments, leading to adjustments in critical dimensions for improved part accuracy.

I employed "tree supports" to minimize filament consumption and enhance surface finish.

Section 7: Electronics - The Nervous System

I implemented an Arduino microcontroller and ULN2003 driver to control a 28BYJ-48 stepper motor, enabling testing of individual joints within the robot arm.

I am currently in the process of creating an electrical system to operate a 28BYJ-48 stepper motor until a limit switch has been pressed, allowing for precise calibration of the robot arm.
